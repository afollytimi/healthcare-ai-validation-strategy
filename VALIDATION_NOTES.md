Validation Strategy and Justification

For the hospital readmission prediction scenario, I propose a hybrid validation approach that combines temporal validation with external validation across partner hospital sites. Temporal validation is essential because patient readmission patterns evolve with seasonal illness trends, evolving clinical guidelines, and shifts in care protocols, so training on data from one period and testing on a strictly later period mirrors how the model will actually be deployed. To complement this, external validation across at least three geographically and demographically distinct hospitals confirms generalisability beyond the development site, which is critical when patient populations, coding practices, and electronic health record systems differ. Stratified sampling is layered into both stages to preserve representation across age bands, comorbidity burden, insurance status, and racial and ethnic groups, ensuring the model is not silently optimised for the majority subgroup. This combined strategy directly addresses the most dangerous failure modes in clinical AI, namely temporal drift and site specific bias, while aligning the validation design with the eventual real world deployment context where the model must perform consistently across heterogeneous patient cohorts and changing clinical environments over time.

Metrics Interpretation and Sample Size Analysis

Interpreting validation metrics for a readmission model requires reading them together rather than in isolation. Suppose the model returns an accuracy of 0.88, precision of 0.72, recall of 0.54, and AUC of 0.81. The high accuracy is misleading because readmission is an imbalanced outcome, where most patients are not readmitted, so a model that under predicts the positive class still looks strong on accuracy. The low recall is the most concerning signal because it means nearly half of the patients who will actually be readmitted are missed, which translates directly into preventable harm and avoidable cost. The gap between AUC and recall also suggests the decision threshold is poorly calibrated for clinical use, and the precision recall imbalance may reflect overfitting to majority class patterns or underrepresentation of high risk subgroups. Recommended actions include threshold tuning to favour recall, cost sensitive learning, resampling the minority class, and subgroup performance audits.

For sample size, using a standard proportion based formula with a 95 percent confidence level, expected event rate of 15 percent, and a 3 percent margin of error, n equals approximately 1.96 squared times 0.15 times 0.85 divided by 0.03 squared, yielding roughly 544 patients per validation site. Inflating this by 20 percent for missingness and subgroup analyses produces a target of around 650 per site, ensuring statistically reliable estimates across diverse cohorts.

Calibration and Report Template Summary

Calibration plots compare predicted probabilities against observed event frequencies across deciles of risk, and a well calibrated model hugs the diagonal closely. Systematic deviation above the line indicates the model underestimates risk, which is particularly dangerous in readmission prediction because high risk patients would be discharged without adequate follow up, while overestimation wastes scarce care management resources. The validation report template I would adopt contains an executive summary, scenario and intended use statement, dataset description with demographic breakdown, validation methodology covering temporal and external splits, performance metrics with subgroup tables, calibration assessment with Brier score and reliability plots, fairness audit, limitations, and a regulatory compliance section mapped to FDA Good Machine Learning Practice and HIPAA, supporting transparent clinical review, audit readiness, and safe equitable deployment.
